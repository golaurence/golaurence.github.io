Political Interdependence: Evidence from Migrant Turnout in 1,267 Elections Worldwide

Journal of the European Economic Association · Forthcoming

Voting in your host country makes you vote back home.

Abstract

We document political interdependence driven by international migration. Assembling a novel dataset of migrant turnout in 1,267 elections across 43 origin and 217 destination countries, and using the quasi-random timing of elections between countries, migrant turnout in homeland elections held after a host-country election increases by 7 percentage points. This is consistent with a salience model: exposure to competitive host-country elections raises interest and drives migrants to participate in their homeland elections.

Inattentive Citizens: Impact of Elections on Political Interest across 93 Countries

Journal of Development Economics · 2026

Elections spike political interest — then it fades within ten days.

Abstract

Using global survey data spanning 93 countries and over one million respondents, political interest rises by roughly 10% after an election, then dissipates within about ten days. The response is stronger when the first round is decisive, when an incumbent is defeated, and when economic policy uncertainty is high — consistent with citizens acquiring costly political information when returns are greatest.

When Running for Office Runs in the Family: Horizontal Dynasties, Policy and Development in the Philippines

Comparative Political Studies · 2022

When relatives split the offices, spending rises — but development doesn’t.

Abstract

We examine horizontal dynasties — families simultaneously holding multiple different offices. Using a regression discontinuity design around close mayoral elections in the Philippines, mayors from horizontal dynasties spend more public resources, driven by weaker institutional checks when relatives occupy neighbouring offices. Despite higher spending, we find no improvements in economic growth or poverty reduction.

First Among Equals: The First Place Effect and Political Promotion in Multi-Member Plurality Elections

Journal of Public Economics · 2021

Coming first — not merely winning — makes politicians run again.

Abstract

We study rank-based decision-making in a multi-member plurality electoral system, comparing narrowly-ranked first- and second-placers among Philippine legislative councilors. First-placers are 5–9 percentage points more likely to run for higher office and 1–4 points more likely to win. The effect is specific to placing first and is not explained by party alignment, strategic voting, incumbency resources, or media exposure.

Absence Makes the Vote Grow Farther: Emigrant Voting Patterns Across 180 Homeland Elections

American Journal of Political Science · Accepted

The greater the political distance, the more emigrants drift from home.

Abstract

Do emigrants change their political preferences from afar? Drawing on a novel dataset of emigrant votes by country of residence across 180 homeland elections worldwide, we estimate pairwise fixed-effects regressions and show that political distance — the gap in Polity scores between origin and residence — systematically relates to divergence between the emigrant and homeland vote. The result is largely driven by migrants moving to more democratic countries.

Pride and Prejudice: Impact of National Days on Patriotism and Attitudes Toward Immigrants

Draft in preparation

National days cool nationalism where countries are ethnically divided.

Abstract

What drives nationalism? Leveraging variation in the timing of national days relative to political survey dates, we find national days reduce nationalism in ethnically fragmented countries, and examine how this shapes public attitudes toward migration.

Development / Politics

Economic Nationalism and Industrial Development: Evidence from the 1975 Naturalization Law

Draft in preparation

A 1975 law opening Philippine industry to foreigners, as a lens on economic nationalism.

Abstract

As economic nationalism gains ground globally, its consequences remain unclear. We study a 1975 Philippine naturalization law that opened protected industries to foreign investors, showing how economic nationalism shapes industry growth, economic development, and market outcomes.

Migrant Workers and Labor Market Institutions in Spain

Draft in preparation

How Spain’s bargaining agreements shape migrant wages — and migration itself.

Abstract

How do labor institutions shape the experiences of migrant workers? Using detailed administrative data, we study how collective bargaining agreements in Spain affect migrant workers’ wages, welfare, and migration decisions — and how labor protections influence migration patterns.
